Abstract

Background: Obesity affects 40.5% of U.S. adults and drives substantial morbidity and healthcare expenditure.6 Identifying modifiable predictors — physical activity, sedentary time, dietary quality — is essential for public health intervention design.

Methods: We analysed cross-sectional data from 6,235 adults (≥18 years) in the CDC NHANES August 2021–August 2023 cycle. Five relational XPT files were merged on respondent ID: demographics (DEMO_L), body measures (BMX_L), physical activity (PAQ_L), dietary recall day 1 (DR1TOT_L), and blood pressure (BPXO_L). Physical activity was quantified as MET-minutes per week using CDC-aligned formulas. Logistic regression with stepwise selection identified independent predictors of obesity (BMI ≥30). Survey-weighted estimates used PROC SURVEYFREQ with MEC weights.1,7

Results: Obesity prevalence was 40.5%. Sedentary adults had 52% obesity vs. 33% in active adults (p<0.001). The composite activity score was the strongest predictor (OR=0.932, p<0.001), followed by sedentary time (OR=1.001/min, p<0.001) and dietary fiber (OR=0.983/g/day, p<0.001). A pronounced income gradient was observed (OR=1.55, below-poverty vs. high-income, p<0.001). The 7-predictor stepwise model achieved AUC=0.656 with outstanding calibration (Hosmer–Lemeshow p=0.990).5

Conclusions: Physical activity, sedentary behavior, dietary fiber, socioeconomic status, and hypertension are the strongest independent modifiable predictors of adult obesity. These findings support multi-component public health interventions targeting sitting time, diet quality, and income-related health disparities.

Section 1

Introduction

Obesity is one of the most consequential public health challenges in the United States. Data from the most recent NHANES cycle (August 2021–August 2023) confirm that 40.5% of American adults have obesity (BMI ≥30 kg/m²), while a further 31.5% are overweight.6 Obesity is a primary driver of type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular disease, hypertension, sleep apnoea, and several cancers, accounting for an estimated $173 billion annually in direct medical costs.

Physical inactivity and poor dietary habits are well-recognised modifiable risk factors. The 2018 Physical Activity Guidelines for Americans recommend at least 150 minutes of moderate-intensity or 75 minutes of vigorous-intensity aerobic activity per week (≥600 MET-minutes/week).2 Yet nearly half of adults fail to meet these guidelines, and prolonged sedentary time has increased independently as screen-based work and leisure have expanded.4

This analysis addresses three research questions: (1) Do physically active adults have lower obesity prevalence than sedentary adults? (2) Which dietary factors are independently associated with obesity after demographic adjustment? (3) Can a parsimonious logistic model predict obesity from behavioral and demographic variables, and how well is it calibrated?

Section 2

Data & Methods

2.1 Data source

Data were drawn from the CDC National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey (NHANES), cycle August 2021–August 2023. NHANES combines household interviews, standardised physical examinations, and laboratory assessments in a probability sample of the U.S. civilian non-institutionalised population.1 All files are freely available in SAS transport (XPT) format at wwwn.cdc.gov/nchs/nhanes.

2.2 Linked datasets

Five XPT files were imported and merged on respondent sequence number (SEQN): demographics (DEMO_L), body measures (BMX_L), physical activity (PAQ_L — 2021–2023 redesigned variable structure), 24-hour dietary recall day 1 (DR1TOT_L), and oscillometric blood pressure (BPXO_L).

2.3 Physical activity quantification

The 2021–2023 PAQ_L questionnaire uses a frequency-unit-duration structure for self-reported leisure activity. MET-minutes/week were calculated as:

MET-min/week = (Moderate min/week × 4.0) + (Vigorous min/week × 8.0)

Adults were classified as: Active (≥600 MET-min/week), Low Active (<600 MET-min/week with some activity), or Sedentary (zero reported leisure activity).

2.4 Statistical analysis

Three logistic regression models were fitted: Model A (physical activity predictors, N=6,235), Model B (dietary predictors, N=4,929 with complete dietary data), and Model C (full stepwise model, p=0.05 entry/stay, N=4,929). Model performance was assessed via AUC, Hosmer–Lemeshow goodness-of-fit (10 groups), decile calibration, and Youden's J optimal cut-off. Survey-weighted prevalence estimates used PROC SURVEYFREQ with WTMEC2YR weights.5,7 All analyses were conducted in SAS 9.4 (SAS OnDemand for Academics).

Section 3

Sample Characteristics

The analytic dataset includes 6,235 adults (mean age 52.3 years, SD 18.2; 54.8% female). The sample was predominantly Non-Hispanic White (58.4%), followed by Non-Hispanic Black (12.2%) and Hispanic (17.3% combined). Obesity prevalence was 40.5% (N=2,524); a further 31.5% were overweight.

Section 5

Logistic Regression Models

5.1 Model A — Physical activity predictors (N=6,235, AUC=0.607)

The composite activity score was the single strongest predictor (OR=0.953/point, p=0.003). Meeting CDC PA guidelines was independently protective (OR=0.694, p=0.013). Sedentary minutes per day raised obesity odds independently of the activity score (OR=1.001/min, p<0.001).

5.2 Model B — Dietary predictors (N=4,929, AUC=0.573)

Dietary fiber was the only significant protective dietary predictor (OR=0.973/g/day, p<0.001). Sodium was positively associated with obesity (OR=1.000/mg, p<0.001), reflecting an overall dietary-pattern effect. Log-caloric intake showed a marginal negative association (OR=0.778, p=0.032).

Section 7

Discussion

Physical activity as the dominant modifiable factor

The composite activity score — the first variable selected in stepwise modelling with the largest Wald chi-square (91.9) — underscores that the full quantity of leisure-time physical activity is a more powerful predictor than either sedentary time alone or dietary variables. Adults in the Sedentary category had a 52% obesity rate versus 33% in the Active group, a difference of nearly 19 percentage points (p<0.001). These findings strongly support population-level efforts to increase leisure-time physical activity as a primary obesity prevention strategy.

Sedentary time as an independent risk factor

After adjusting for the activity score, sedentary minutes per day remained an independent, significant predictor (OR=1.001/min, p<0.001). Adults averaging 480 min/day of sedentary time have approximately 12% higher odds of obesity than those averaging 360 min/day, independent of their leisure activity. This confirms the emerging consensus that prolonged sitting is a distinct health risk, not simply the absence of exercise.4

Dietary fiber

Among all dietary factors, only fiber was a robust independent predictor of lower obesity odds (OR=0.983/g/day). Adults consuming the recommended 35 g/day versus the national median of 14 g/day would have approximately 37% lower odds of obesity, all else equal. With 42.8% of adults below 15 g/day, there is substantial population-level headroom for improvement through dietary intervention.3

Socioeconomic gradient

The persistent income gradient — with below-poverty adults having 55% higher obesity odds than high-income peers, even after adjusting for activity, diet, and demographics — reflects structural barriers including food insecurity, limited access to safe recreational spaces, time constraints, and stress-mediated eating. This finding underscores the need for upstream, structural interventions alongside individual behavior change programs.

Limitations

Limitations

  • Cross-sectional design. Causality cannot be inferred from these data. Longitudinal designs would be required to establish temporal ordering of exposures and outcomes.
  • Single-day dietary recall. DR1TOT_L captures one 24-hour period, subject to day-to-day variability and recall bias. Two-day averaged estimates would be preferable.
  • Self-reported physical activity. PAQ_L is subject to social desirability bias; accelerometer-based objective data are available in alternate NHANES cycles.
  • Missing dietary data. 1,301 participants (20.9%) lacked complete dietary data and were excluded from Models B and C, introducing potential selection bias.
  • BMI as outcome. BMI does not directly measure body fatness and may misclassify individuals with high lean mass or atypical fat distribution.
  • Moderate discrimination. AUC=0.656 reflects the inherent limitation of self-reported behavioral data for predicting a continuously distributed trait.
